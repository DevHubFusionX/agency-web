Executive summary
Nigeria’s product-development market is large and crowded with many small agencies and
freelancers; opportunity exists for a product-thinking, MVP-first, outcome-oriented agency
that sells credibility (portfolio + process), predictable delivery, and post-launch growth/retainer
services — that’s NEMVOL’s sweet spot.

1) Market context — facts that matter
 The global software development outsourcing market is large and still growing (est.
~USD 564B in 2025 with high single-digit CAGR), which keeps demand for external
product teams robust. (Mordor Intelligence)
 Nigeria’s startup & fintech ecosystem remains a major demand driver (sustained funding,
fintech growth, large payments volumes), creating steady MVP and product work for
local agencies. (TC Insights)
 There are dozens — if not hundreds — of web/app/product agencies and consultants in

Nigeria listed across directories (Clutch, TechBehemoths, local lists) and many rapid-
growth specialist studios prioritizing mobile/web apps and MVPs. This means high local

competition but also wide choice for clients. (Clutch)
 Market behavior: founders increasingly seek MVPs that validate one problem (not
feature bloat); agencies that can deliver fast, cost-efficient, measurable MVPs win
traction. Local content on MVP best practice and demand is growing. (PlanetWeb
Solutions)

2) Competitive landscape — map & player types
Group competitors into four practical buckets:
1. Large/full-service agencies (Brand + Tech + Scale)
o Examples: top-ranked Lagos agencies (listed on Clutch, Topdevelopers).
Strengths: stronger processes, enterprise clients, marketing + delivery.
Weaknesses: higher price, slower for early startups. (Clutch)

2. Product & MVP studios (specialist teams)
o Smaller teams that focus on fast MVP builds and product design (Appfur, Ecagon,
Softcom-type players). Strengths: product focus, speed, founder empathy.
Weaknesses: limited capacity, variable post-launch support. (Appfur)

3. Boutique design/development shops & freelancers
o Low cost, flexible. Strengths: cheap, fast. Weaknesses: quality inconsistency,
limited governance, IP/contract risk. Directory listings and freelance marketplaces
show robust supply. (Twine)

4. Offshore/outsourced vendors (outside Nigeria) taking African contracts
o Global outsourcing firms and remote teams competing on price and specialized
skillsets. Strengths: scale, certain tech stacks. Threat: price competition and
tendency to undercut local firms. (Mordor Intelligence)

Competitive attributes to compare (for NEMVOL’s positioning): price, speed, product
thinking (discovery & UX), portfolio depth, retainer services, delivery guarantees (SLA), quality
assurance, post-launch growth support (analytics/ops), IP/legal clarity, client references.

3) What customers (founders & SMEs) are telling us — pain
points
(derived from sector reports, blog posts, and community signals)
 Feature bloat & overbuilding: many founders build too much before validating; they
want lean MVP support. (PlanetWeb Solutions)
 Poor delivery discipline: scope creep, missed timelines, poor communication. (frequent
complaint in reviews across agency listings). (Clutch)
 Unclear ownership of IP and contractual risk with freelancers or small shops.
(common ask in legal/sme guidance). (Fate Foundation)
 Lack of post-launch growth help: agencies build product then hand off; founders need
product ops, analytics, and customer acquisition support. (Novatia Consulting)

 Talent & infrastructure gaps: hiring senior devs is hard; clients need teams that
combine senior product thinking with junior builders. (Betternship)

4) Market gaps — where NEMVOL can win (high-
opportunity whitespaces)

These are prioritized by business impact and feasibility for a new agency:
1. MVP-first, metric-driven offering with fixed outcomes
o Offer ―MVP in 8–10 weeks‖ packages with clearly defined success metrics
(activation, retention targets). Gap: many agencies sell features, not outcomes.

2. End-to-end product + growth retainers (build → measure → iterate)
o Most shops stop at deployment. Offering a combined ―launch + 3-month growth‖
retainer (analytics, CRO, small experiments) differentiates NEMVOL.

3. Transparent, modular pricing & milestone SLAs
o Few agencies publish simple packaged pricing; startups want price predictability
and enforcement of timelines.
4. Product discovery & investor pack service
o Combine discovery, prototypes, and investor-grade pitch/MVP decks so founders
can both test and fundraise.

5. Quality assurance + compliance as a standard
o Offer IP ownership clauses, data protection guidance, and basic compliance
checklists as standard deliverables.
6. Localised UX expertise for African contexts
o Deep understanding of payment flows, low-bandwidth design, USSD fallback,
and offline experiences — many global agencies lack this nuance.

7. AI-augmented development stack (speed + cost)
o Use of AI tools to speed development and reduce costs while keeping human
oversight — early adopters will gain margin advantage as rates compress. (But be
cautious: clients still value human accountability.)

8. Talent surrogate model (on-demand senior product leads + local dev teams)
o Provide fractional Chief Product Officers or senior PMs to lead client projects; fill
execution with local dev talent.

5) Benchmarked competitor strengths & weaknesses (high
level)
(Composite view from Clutch/agency directories and niche lists)

 Top full-service agencies: strong branding, corporate clients, but expensive and slow.
Good for enterprise work; weak for early-stage founders. (Clutch)
 MVP studios: fast and product aware, but capacity limited and seldom offer growth
retainers. (Appfur)
 Freelancers: low cost but inconsistent quality, limited governance and legal protection
for clients. (Twine)
 Offshore vendors: often cheaper per hour and scalable, but may lack local market UX
and post-launch support. (Mordor Intelligence)

7) GTM & pricing recommendations (practical)
1. Signature packages (publish on website):
o Discovery Sprint (2 weeks, fixed price) → deliver validated prototypes + investor
pack.
o MVP Express (8–12 weeks, milestone payments) → functional product +
analytics + 30-day support.
o Launch & Grow Retainer (3 months) → analytics, CRO experiments, small
feature roadmap.
2. Pricing rules of thumb
o Discovery: low fixed fee to lower entry friction (e.g., $500–$2,000 local
equivalence).
o MVP: tiered — Starter MVP, Growth MVP, Enterprise MVP. Publish ranges to
pre-qualify leads.
o Retainers: monthly fee based on outcomes (e.g., growth retainer = % of agreed
marketing/ad budget or fixed fee).

8) Delivery model & ops — how to keep quality high while
scaling
 1 senior PM per two active projects (fractional CPO model).
 Standardized delivery playbook: discovery template, sprint plans, acceptance criteria,
QA checklists, deployment runbooks.
 Milestone SLAs: payments tied to acceptance; milestones include functional demos and
user-testing results.
 Use of AI tools: for boilerplate code, tests, and prototyping — but retain human code
review and accountability. (This reduces cost and time; watch quality.) (The Times of
India)
 Post-launch KPIs: set quantitative product OKRs (activation, retention, revenue) and tie
retainer scope to those metrics.

9) Financial & capacity considerations (starter model)
 Low cash burn approach: founder as sales + PM for first 3 months; contract
devs/designer per project.
 Break-even target: secure 3–5 paid MVPs or 2 retainers within first 6 months.
 Tools & stack: Figma (design), GitHub, cloud hosting (AWS/GCP/DigitalOcean),
Jira/Trello, analytics (Amplitude/GA4/Mixpanel), CI/CD.

10) KPIs to track (first 12 months)
 Number of paid Discovery Sprints per month
 Conversion rate Discovery → MVP

 Average MVP build time (target 8–12 weeks)
 Client NPS / satisfaction score
 Retainer renewal rate
 Gross margin per project

11) Actionable 90-day launch plan (priority tasks)
Week 1–2: finalize brand + one-page website; publish 3 packages and sample pricing; create
contract + IP + SLA templates.
Week 3–6: run 4 free Discovery Clinics; convert 2 paid Discoveries; hire 1 senior PM
(fractional).
Week 7–12: deliver first 1–2 MVPs; publish one case study and client testimonial; onboard first
retainer client.

12) Risks & mitigations (brief)
 Price competition — mitigate with outcome guarantees, packaged scope, and faster time
to launch.
 Talent shortage — mitigate with a vetted contractor bench and fractional senior hires.
(Novatia Consulting)
 AI disruption — embrace AI to improve margin but keep human oversight; highlight
accountability and code quality in sales. (The Times of India)

case studies.

price.

leads.

Appendix — SWOT summary (compact)
Strengths
 Product-first positioning (Discovery → MVP → Growth).
 Local UX + payments + offline experience expertise.
 Low overheads with contractor bench — scalable and cost-efficient.
Weaknesses
 New brand (no track record yet).
 Limited initial capacity and cash runway.
 Need to build credible case studies quickly.
Opportunities
 High demand from Nigerian fintechs, marketplaces, and SMEs for MVPs and fast
product iterations. (TC Insights)
 Growing outsourcing demand globally & regionally; local agencies can capture work
from Africa and diaspora startups. (Mordor Intelligence)
 Many agencies don’t bundle post-launch growth and analytics — opportunity to capture
recurring revenue.
Threats
 Intense local competition (many agencies & freelancers). (Clutch)
 Race to the bottom on price from offshore vendors. (Mordor Intelligence)
 Rapid tooling/AI changes could compress developer rates and change delivery
economics. (The Times of India)
